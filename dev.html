01 — Overview

About the Challenge

Radiological reporting of non-contrast head CT scans is a cornerstone of acute neurological care. In emergency settings, clinicians rely on fast, precise, and comprehensive reports to guide life-critical decisions. Yet this process remains time-intensive and highly dependent on expert interpretation.

The HEADLINE MICCAI 2026 challenge introduces a dedicated benchmark for automated report generation from 3D head CT scans using Vision-Language Models.

03 — Data & Tasks

Dataset & Tasks

The HEADLINE dataset comprises a large-scale collection of real-world, non-contrast head CT scans paired with corresponding radiologist-authored clinical reports. All cases originate from routine clinical practice and capture the full spectrum and complexity of emergency neuroradiology, including both normal findings and a wide range of pathologies such as haemorrhage, ischaemia, trauma, and tumours.

Each case includes the complete 3D volumetric CT scan with soft tissue and bone reconstructions, reflecting standard clinical reading conditions. The reference standard is defined by expert-validated radiology reports, ensuring a high level of diagnostic accuracy.

All data undergo rigorous de-identification and quality control. The dataset is specifically designed to reflect real-world variability in imaging protocols, scanner types, and reporting styles, thereby enabling the development of robust, generalisable, and clinically relevant AI systems.

Task: Report Generation

Given a full 3D volumetric CT image, models must produce a clinically accurate, coherent, and structured free-text report - identifying and describing relevant findings such as haemorrhage, infarction, fractures, or mass lesions, along with their anatomical location, extent, and clinical significance.

04 — Evaluation

Evaluation Metrics

Submissions are evaluated using a two-stage, clinically grounded framework combining automated metrics and expert review. This hybrid evaluation ensures that top-ranking models are not only linguistically strong, but also clinically safe and meaningful.

In the first stage, all models are assessed using a weighted composite score based on: BERTScore (F1), METEOR, ROUGE-L, and BLEU-4. In the second stage, the top-performing models are evaluated by board-certified radiologists using a Clinical Utility Score, focusing on clinical accuracy, completeness, and potential for harm.

07 — FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Who can participate?

The challenge is open to researchers, clinicians, and industry participants from both academia and commercial organisations. Interdisciplinary teams are highly encouraged.

Is the dataset publicly available?

The dataset is not publicly available. After registration, participants will receive a Data Transfer and Usage Agreement (DUA). Signing this agreement is mandatory to gain access to the dataset and participate in the challenge. Once the signed agreement has been reviewed and approved, participants will receive access to download the training dataset. The dataset is provided exclusively for use within the scope of this challenge and must not be used for any commercial purposes.

Can I use external data or pre-trained models?

Yes. Participants are allowed to use publicly available external datasets and pre-trained models. This includes foundation models and open-source resources, as long as they are accessible to all participants at the time of the challenge.

What are the computational resource requirements?

All submissions must be provided as Docker containers and will be executed on the standardised infrastructure of the evaluation platform. Models need to run within predefined limits of runtime, memory, and compute resources. Submissions exceeding these limits may be terminated and treated as failed cases. This setup ensures a fair, reproducible, and secure evaluation environment for all participants. The detailed specifications of the computational constraints will be published here in the coming days.
